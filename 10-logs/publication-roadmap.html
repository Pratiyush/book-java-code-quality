1 The journey so far — in sequence

Every stage below is actually complete and has evidence on disk (drafts, gate reports, rendered figures, green builds). The pipeline ran largely autonomously through five locked phases; only the whole-book release gate is reserved for a human.

  1. 0
    Phase 0 · Foundation
    Strategy, source pin & candidate pool
    Set the charter (senior Java audience, neutral survey, runnable companion code). Pinned a multi-authority source set in SOURCE-PIN.md (Java 21 + 25 LTS anchor; every tool pinned per row, web-verified). Built CANDIDATE_POOL.md — 110 frozen-key topics across 15 parts. Every fact in the book must trace back to this pin.
  2. 1
    Phase 1 · Research
    Banked a verified dossier per candidate
    All 110/110 dossiers researched and banked. Keys 07–40 went through a multi-agent workflow each with its own _VERIFY.md; the rest via a leaner main-loop pass. Global neutrality sweep across the pool returned 0 banned phrasings.
  3. 2
    Phase 2 · Select
    Scored the pool, culled to ONE book — human-confirmed & LOCKED
    The 110 keys consolidated by merge (not by hard cuts) into a tight 47-chapter / 14-part volume. FINAL_INDEX.md was confirmed and LOCKED by the author on 2026-06-20; it now gates all drafting. A re-cull requires a deliberate human re-latch.
  4. 3
    Phase 3 · Draft
    Drafted all 47 chapters in the locked voice
    Every chapter drafted from its banked dossier in the one locked third-person voice, then voice-lifted chapter by chapter for consistency. Each claim is bound to a pinned source; untraceable atoms were flagged, never invented.
  5. 4
    Phase 3 · Figures
    Designed & rendered 67 figures (HTML→PNG, never image-generated)
    Load-bearing diagrams authored as HTML then rendered to cropped PNGs via the render.mjs + figures.css asset chain; tool/IDE screenshots captured per the figure guide. All 67/67 figures pass the 20-parameter figure review.
  6. 5
    Phase 3 · Companion code
    Built runnable companion modules + 5 capstone apps
    A single Maven reactor (08-companion-code/) with 45 chapter modules + 2 honest N/A (pure-concept chapters), plus 5 enterprise capstone apps (shared platform + commerce, fintech ledger, logistics saga, quality-operations). The whole reactor builds mvn -Pquality verify → BUILD SUCCESS, 0 Checkstyle / 0 SpotBugs.
  7. 6
    Phase 3 · EXAMPLE-BUILD & CODE-REVIEW
    FLOOR-C closed book-wide (compile + code-review)
    Every displayed snippet is a // tag:: region inside a file that compiles green. The CODE-REVIEW half ran on all 45 modules → 45 _CODEREVIEW.md reports, 45 PASS / 0 FAIL. FLOOR-C is the first content floor fully closed for every chapter that ships code.
  8. 7
    Phase 3 · Source-verify
    Traced facts to the pin; flagged what needs a network
    Bounded web source-verify of public-spec residuals (JEP/ISO/OWASP). Roughly 182 @pin residuals (copyrighted-book verbatims and live-SaaS rows) remain legitimately flagged and dated-at-use, awaiting a networked /pin-source pass.
  9. 8
    Phase 3 · Independent scoring
    Harsh, independent Opus score on all 47 chapters
    Each chapter scored by a separate model against the 5-cluster rubric + the A/B/C content floors. Routing: an independent score ≥88% with floors PASS → auto-approve into 04-approved/; below the bar → a bounded lift loop (≤3 passes). To date 29 chapters auto-approved; the remainder are floor-clean but capped on flagged source atoms or held for the human gate.
  10. 9
    Phase 3 · Pre-final QA
    Clean review copy + manuscript-gate dry-runs
    Assembled a clean pre-final/ review copy and ran four manuscript-level dry-runs (independent model): ORIGINALITY PASS (0 regurgitation), PROOF + REDTEAM PASS-WITH-FIXES, READERSIM FIX. Findings folded into a REVIEW-FINDINGS.md prefinal-edit worklist (figure-number normalization, metadata leaks, a handful of factual reconciliations).

Tooling built along the way

The pipeline runs on purpose-built tooling, not by hand:

  • status.py — rewrites the status matrix + scoring/approval pages + 5 HTML dashboards, runs the anti-drift guard, and applies auto-approval at the 88% bar.
  • build_prefinal.py — assembles the clean external-review copy.
  • review_figures.py — the 20-parameter figure-review gate → figures.html.
  • The 5 dashboards: dashboard, chapters, scoring, figures, capstones, + an audit log.
  • An audit hook / trail (audit.jsonl) recording every gate at its real date.

QA catches that mattered

The gates earned their keep by catching real defects:

  • FLOOR-C / source-trace fixes (~14): two NEUTRALITY ("beats" / "better than") catches — one inside a shipped code comment, not just prose — plus broken snippet tags, a wrong boundary operator, and an off-pin JaCoCo version.
  • The false-approval parser bug: status.py was mis-reading gate verdicts (PASS-while-counting-failures, FAIL→upgraded lines), which under-credited FLOOR-C and risked mis-routing. Fixed to follow upgrades and take the last decisive verdict.
  • Book-wide cross-reference renumber (54 fixes; 0 in-prose refs > 47 remain).

3 Market analysis & approximate sales

An honest, conservative read of the self-published technical-book market. The headline: most self-published books sell modestly — this section deliberately under-promises. Every figure is dated and attributed; do not treat these as guarantees.

The market reality

  • The often-cited average self-published book sells ~250 copies over its lifetime, and ~90% sell fewer than 100 copies — the average is skewed by a few outliers. (WordsRated, "Self-Published Book Sales Statistics," accessed 2026-06-28)
  • For non-fiction, traditionally published titles have a median around 7,100 copies — but that reflects publisher distribution this book will not have. (Mary Adkins / industry summary, accessed 2026-06-28)
  • A realistic lifetime range for a self-published technical book without heavy marketing is ~100–500 copies; marketing is the difference-maker. (synthesis of WordsRated + Bernoff, accessed 2026-06-28)

Platform & royalty models

  • Amazon KDP — ebook: 70% royalty only between $2.99–$9.99 (minus a small per-MB delivery fee); 35% outside that band. (KDP Help / AuthorImprints, accessed 2026-06-28)
  • KDP — print: 60% of list minus print cost ($9.99+); as of Jun 10 2025, titles ≤$9.98 earn 50%. Print cost for a 400+ page code book is substantial. (AuthorImprints / KDP, accessed 2026-06-28)
  • Leanpub: 80% royalty ($7.99+; minus $0.50 under that). Variable/reader-set pricing; popular with technical authors and in-progress books. (Leanpub Help Center, accessed 2026-06-28)
  • Traditional publisher: typically ~10% of a ~$32–$50 list (often less on ebooks) but with editing, distribution, and credibility. (Leanpub blog comparison, accessed 2026-06-28)
Genre anchor, not a sales comp: Effective Java (Bloch, 3rd ed., Addison-Wesley, 2017) is the category's reference and ranks high in Amazon's Java sub-category — but it is traditionally published by a famous author with two prior editions. It sets the bar for quality and topic interest, not for what a first-time self-published title should expect to sell. (O'Reilly / Amazon listing, accessed 2026-06-28)

The AI-authored angle — marketing and disclosure

As marketing: a rigorously gated, fully AI-produced technical book is itself a story — it can differentiate the launch and appeal to readers curious about AI-assisted authorship. As disclosure: it must be stated honestly. KDP asks whether content is AI-generated; the book already carries an AI-DISCLOSURE.md. Some readers and reviewers weigh AI authorship negatively, so a visible human review + technical-verification story (every code example compiles; every fact traced to a pinned source) is the right framing — lead with the rigor, disclose the method.

4 Self-publishing setup & the "do I need a company?" question

GENERAL INFORMATION — NOT LEGAL, TAX, OR HR ADVICE. The author works at Fiserv (a fintech / payments company). The notes below are educational and intentionally caveated. They are not definitive conclusions. Before publishing, consult a qualified attorney and tax professional, and review your own Fiserv employment agreement and code of conduct. Fintech employers commonly have specific outside-activity and IP clauses; only your own documents and advisors can tell you how they apply.

(a) You can publish as an individual

You generally do not need to form a company to self-publish. KDP and Leanpub let you publish as an individual; income is typically reported as self-employment / sole-proprietor income on Schedule C, and you complete a W-9 / tax interview on the platform. (Keeper / TurboTax author guides, accessed 2026-06-28)

(b) When an LLC starts to make sense

Sole-proprietor is simplest and cheapest. An LLC (or other entity) becomes worth considering as the project scales — for liability separation, cleaner IP ownership, potential tax elections, or if you take on co-authors, sell courses, or run paid ads at volume. For a single book it is usually optional, not required. (general small-business / moonlighting guidance, accessed 2026-06-28)

(c) The employment angle — the part that matters most here

Because the author is at a fintech employer, this deserves the most care. Fintech and large tech employers commonly include outside-activity / moonlighting-disclosure clauses and broad IP-assignment clauses in their agreements. A book written on personal time about general industry knowledge (Java code quality) — not Fiserv work, systems, data, or IP — is typically fine, but "typically" is not "certainly." Reasonable, commonly recommended precautions:

  • Review the Fiserv employment agreement & code of conduct for outside-activity, moonlighting, non-compete, and invention/IP-assignment language. (Some agreements are broad; some states, e.g. California Labor Code §2870, limit employer claims to work unrelated to the employer's business and done without its assets. (LegalGPS / moonlighting-IP guidance, accessed 2026-06-28))
  • Consider a written outside-activity disclosure to HR/manager — a short note that you are writing a general-knowledge technical book on personal time can pre-empt later questions and is often explicitly required.
  • Keep all work on personal equipment and personal time — no employer laptop, accounts, networks, or work hours. Using employer assets is a classic way to muddy IP ownership.
  • Use no Fiserv confidential material — no internal systems, code, data, customer info, or anything that could be read as proprietary or payments-specific. Keep the content generic and publicly-sourced (which the source-pin discipline already enforces).

This is risk-reduction, not a legal clearance. Only your own agreement + an attorney can confirm your situation.

(d) Tax basics

  • Royalty income from active self-publishing is generally self-employment income on Schedule C, subject to the ~15.3% SE tax in addition to income tax. (Monaco CPA / TurboTax, accessed 2026-06-28)
  • 1099 / W-9: Amazon issues a 1099-MISC for as little as $10/yr in royalties; complete the platform's W-9 / tax interview at signup. (Keeper Tax, accessed 2026-06-28)
  • Quarterly estimates: platforms don't withhold, so you may owe quarterly estimated taxes (federal safe harbor: 100% of prior-year tax, 110% if prior-year AGI > $150k). Your W-2 day-job withholding can sometimes absorb modest royalties — ask your tax professional. (Arc & Ledger author tax guide, accessed 2026-06-28)
  • Deductions: ordinary business expenses (cover design, editing, ISBN, ads) reduce net profit on Schedule C — keep records and receipts.
Short answer to "do I need to register a company?"  No — you can publish as an individual / sole-proprietor and report royalties on Schedule C. Form an LLC later only if liability, tax, or scale justifies it. The more pressing item is not entity formation — it is reviewing your Fiserv employment agreement and considering a written outside-activity disclosure, keeping all work on personal time/equipment with no confidential material. Confirm specifics with an attorney and a tax professional.
