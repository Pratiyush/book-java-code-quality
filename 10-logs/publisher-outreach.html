0 Traditional vs self-publish — the honest trade

The decision is not "more money." Self-publishing pays a far higher per-unit royalty; a traditional publisher trades that for editing, distribution, credibility, and reach. Pick the path that matches the goal.

What a traditional publisher gives

  • Credibility & brand — a Manning / O'Reilly / Pragmatic spine carries weight with senior engineers and helps conference/speaking visibility.
  • Professional editing — developmental + copyedit + technical review, which an AI-drafted book still benefits from.
  • Distribution & reach — bookstore, library, Safari/O'Reilly-platform, and translation channels a solo author cannot match.
  • An advance — non-refundable money up front (modest for tech, but real).
  • It absorbs production — typeset, cover, EPUB/print, ISBN, marketing scaffolding.

What it costs you

  • Per-unit royalty drops sharply — roughly ~10% of net vs 35–70%+ self-published (see the money section).
  • You give up rights & control — schedule, title, cover, and often a non-compete.
  • It is slower — months of acquisition + a 6–12 month production cycle vs days to self-list.
  • Acceptance is not guaranteed — even a complete manuscript can be passed on.
Recommendation for this book: pursue traditional first, with a hard time-box. The manuscript is complete and independently gated, the niche values authoritative references, and a recognized imprint compounds the author's credibility far more than the royalty delta costs — the realistic self-pub base case is only ~$4k–$9k net anyway (Roadmap §3). Query the top 2–3 houses; if no contract within ~3–4 months, fall back to the ready self-publish path. The two are not mutually exclusive in sequence.

The one-sentence comparison: a traditional tech contract pays roughly ~10% of net (a few dollars a copy) plus a ~$2k–$5k advance; self-publishing pays 35–70%+ per copy (Leanpub up to 80%) but with no advance, no editing, and no distribution. For a niche reference, the realistic self-pub base case is ~$4k–$9k net total (Roadmap §3) — so the traditional advance alone can rival expected self-pub royalties, with credibility on top.
Don't budget on royalties. First-time tech-book authors should expect the advance to be most of the near-term money. The value here is the artifact, the credibility, and the reach — not the cheque.

4 Contract red-flags to watch

If an offer comes, read these clauses before signing. None is necessarily a deal-breaker, but each is negotiable and each has bitten technical authors. Have a publishing attorney or the Authors Guild review the contract — this list is general information, not legal advice.

1 · Rights scope (grant of rights)

Watch for an all-rights, all-languages, all-formats, life-of-copyright grant. Prefer a defined territory/format grant, and ensure subsidiary rights (translation, audio, course/video adaptation) are either retained or fairly split. Clarify who controls the companion code — keep the runnable repo under your own open-source license (proposed Apache-2.0), separate from the prose grant.

2 · Non-compete clause

A broad non-compete can bar you from writing or even blogging/speaking on "Java code quality" for years. Narrow it to a directly-competing full-length book on the same topic; explicitly carve out talks, articles, training, and the open-source companion repo. This matters most given the planned content-marketing.

3 · eBook & platform royalty

eBook and platform-subscription (Safari/O'Reilly-platform) royalties are often lower or vaguely defined ("net" can hide platform splits). Pin down the eBook %, how subscription/"all-you-can-read" reads are counted and paid, and whether deep-discount/bulk sales drop your rate. The Authors Guild benchmark is ~50% of net for ebooks; tech houses usually offer less — know the number.

4 · Out-of-print & rights reversion

In the print-on-demand / ebook era a book is "never out of print," which can lock up your rights forever. Insist on a reversion clause tied to a real revenue/sales floor over a period (not merely "available for sale"), so rights return if the book stops selling. Also check the revised-edition clause — who decides on a new edition, and your royalty/obligation when Java/tools move.

Also worth checking

  • Advance schedule & recoupment — when is the advance paid (signing / delivery / publication), and is it cross-collateralized against other titles? (Avoid cross-collateralization.)
  • Manuscript-acceptance / "satisfactory" clause — a vague "satisfactory to publisher" can let them reject and reclaim the advance; push for an objective acceptance standard.
  • Deadlines & penalties — realistic given a near-complete manuscript, but check late-delivery and termination terms.
  • AI-authorship representations & warranties — you'll likely warrant originality and rights; ensure the AI-assisted disclosure and your verification process are acceptable, and that the warranty language is one you can truthfully make.
  • Audit rights & reporting cadence — the right to audit royalty statements, and at least semi-annual reporting.

  4. 4

  5. 5
    ~Week 5 · early Aug

    If a contract you're comfortable with is in hand → proceed. If not, fall back to the ready self-publish path (the manuscript, figures, and companion code are already built; Roadmap §2). Either way, the book ships.
