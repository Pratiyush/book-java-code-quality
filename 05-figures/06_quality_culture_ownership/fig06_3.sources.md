## Accessibility

- Alt-text: Three-column comparison of strong, weak, and collective code ownership, each with its strengths, costs, when to prefer it, and its mechanism.
- Long-description: A three-column comparison, one column per ownership model, each with rows for how it works, strengths, costs, when to prefer it, and a mechanism. The left column, "Strong ownership — each module has one named owner; only they change it": one person or team owns each module and others must request changes through the owner; strengths are clear accountability, deep expertise, and consistent style; costs are bottlenecks, bus-factor risk, silos, and slow cross-team change; prefer it for small deep specialist areas and long-lived stable APIs; the mechanism is a CODEOWNERS file routing reviews to the owner automatically. The middle column, "Weak ownership — owners exist; others may edit with courtesy": modules have an owner on record but any member may change them with a convention to notify the owner and no hard gate; strengths are retained accountability, enabled flow, and reduced bottlenecks; costs are ambiguity at the edges and courtesy norms eroding under pressure; prefer it as a pragmatic middle ground; the mechanism is a lightweight CODEOWNERS for review suggestions, not enforced blocks. The right column, "Collective ownership — the whole team owns all code": no single owner, any member may change any module, with shared standards and automated gates holding the line; strengths are no bottlenecks, shared knowledge, and a higher bus factor; costs are quality drifting without gates and the need for strong shared standards; prefer it for teams that want flow and shared understanding when the gates support it. A callout in the collective column states that collective ownership requires automated gates — the Checkstyle, PMD, SpotBugs, and quality-gate layers keeping every contributor honest — so culture and tooling are inseparable. A footer note advises choosing deliberately by context and notes that "you build it, you run it" (Werner Vogels, Amazon) is a strong-ownership variant that pushes operational accountability to the team that wrote the code.
- Grayscale-safe: Yes, reinforced. The three columns are distinguished by position and heading text. Within each column the strength pills carry a green outline and the cost pills a red outline, but each pill group also sits under its own labelled "STRENGTHS" or "COSTS" heading and now carries TWO non-color cues: a leading "+" glyph and a solid border on strength pills, and a leading "−" (U+2212) glyph and a dashed border on cost pills. A grayscale or color-vision-deficient reader therefore tells strengths from costs by the +/− glyph, the solid-vs-dashed border, the section heading, and the wording — never by hue. (Prior soft FIX implemented: per-pill non-color cue added.)
- Contrast: Sufficient. Dark ink on light fills; the pill outlines are thin but the pill text is dark and legible at print size.
- Code legibility: The inline CODEOWNERS token is real authored monospace text, not a screenshot, readable without syntax color.
