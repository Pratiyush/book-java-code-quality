Fig 53.1 — SOLID: intent and over-application trap

Five heuristics for low coupling + high cohesion — useful vocabulary, not a law to maximize. No principle is crowned.

SOLID (Martin)
SRP · OCP · LSP · ISP · DIP — principles that aim at low coupling, high cohesion, safe change. Widely taught shared vocabulary; maps to measurable structure.
vs
CUPID (North) & "simple over SOLID"
Composable · Unix-philosophy · Predictable · Idiomatic · Domain-based. Argues dogmatic SOLID drives premature abstraction that hurts readability. Legitimate alternative lens — neither crowned.
Principle Rule of thumb Smell it fights (Java) Over-application trap
SRP
Single Responsibility
 A class should have one reason to change. God service
2 000-line class that does everything 		Cloud of one-method classes — a single behaviour scattered across a dozen files; hurts readability.
OCP
Open/Closed
 Open for extension; closed for modification — new behaviour is a new class, not an edit. Shotgun surgery
One change forcing edits to many existing classes 		Speculative abstraction — an extension point added for a variation that never comes (YAGNI tension).
LSP
Liskov Substitution
 A subtype must be substitutable for its supertype without breaking callers' expectations.
Nearest to a hard correctness rule — ties to equals/hashCode contract and sealed types.
 Broken contract
Subclass that strengthens preconditions or weakens the contract 		Less of an over-application risk; violation is a latent bug. Sealed types make permitted subtypes explicit.
ISP
Interface Segregation
 Many focused role interfaces over one fat interface — a client depends only on methods it uses. Fat interface
Callers forced to implement methods they never use 		Interface proliferation — too many single-method interfaces adds indirection with no real variation.
DIP
Dependency Inversion
 Depend on abstractions, not concretions — program to interfaces; inject implementations. Hard dependency
Class instantiates its own collaborators; untestable 		Five layers of DI wiring to add one field — interface-with-one-implementation satisfies DIP on paper, adds indirection that makes change harder.
Heuristics, not metrics. SOLID-ness cannot be gated — "one reason to change" is subjective; the measurable, enforceable subset is coupling direction and cycles (next chapter). Records, sealed types, and pattern matching (Ch 15) sometimes reach a SOLID goal more directly than a classic refactor.
